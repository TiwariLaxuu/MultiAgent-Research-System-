**Introduction**

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique used in natural language processing (NLP) to improve the
performance of large language models (LLMs). RAG enables LLMs to retrieve and incorporate new information 
from external data sources, reducing the need for frequent retraining and improving their overall 
performance. This report provides an in-depth analysis of two approaches to building RAG systems: 
Traditional RAG and Agentic RAG. The main objective of this report is to explore the differences between 
these two approaches and their applications in NLP.

**Key Findings**

The research gathered indicates that Traditional RAG and Agentic RAG are two distinct approaches to building
RAG systems. Traditional RAG systems are designed to retrieve relevant information from a knowledge base or 
database and use it to generate text. While they are effective at pulling in relevant chunks of information,
they can struggle to understand the context and nuances of human language. On the other hand, Agentic RAG is
a more advanced approach that uses agents to interact with the environment and generate text. This approach 
is designed to be more flexible and adaptable, and can better understand the context and intent behind human
language.

The key differences between Traditional RAG and Agentic RAG are:

1. **Focus**: Traditional RAG is more focused on retrieving relevant information, while Agentic RAG is more 
focused on understanding the context and intent behind the language.
2. **Adaptability**: Traditional RAG is more rigid and less adaptable, while Agentic RAG is more flexible 
and can learn from interactions with the environment.
3. **Suitability**: Traditional RAG is more suitable for tasks that require retrieving specific information,
while Agentic RAG is more suitable for tasks that require generating text that is context-dependent and 
nuanced.

The benefits of RAG, in general, include reducing hallucinations, improving accuracy, and providing 
transparency. However, RAG is not a direct solution to all challenges and may struggle with context, 
interpretation, and conflicting information. Agentic RAG, in particular, has the potential to revolutionize 
the field of NLP by providing a more advanced and sophisticated approach to building RAG systems.

**Conclusion**

In conclusion, Traditional RAG and Agentic RAG are two different approaches to building RAG systems, each 
with its strengths and weaknesses. While Traditional RAG is effective at retrieving relevant information, 
Agentic RAG is more advanced and can better understand the context and intent behind human language. The 
choice between these two approaches depends on the specific task and requirements of the application. 
Further research is needed to fully explore the potential of Agentic RAG and its applications in NLP.
